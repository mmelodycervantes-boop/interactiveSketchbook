Reading Reflection 2

Norman + Nielsen

The heuristic that is most aligned with Norman’s concerns in Chapter 1 is the match between the system and the real world. Norman argues that the design should communicate to the user how it should be used intuitively without overcomplicating the process. The user should be able to discover what is possible within the design and should know how things work due to the design. In Nielsen's article, specifically with the match between the system and the real world, Nielsen suggests that the system should be familiar to the users, in the sense that it appears natural and in a logical order. The user should understand words, phrases, and processes without having to research anything. Both readings focus on how good design should be familiar and easy to understand, such as the push and pull door example that Norman gives, where people end up pushing when they should pull based on prior experiences.

Challenging My Own Work

The two heuristics that my micro project currently struggles with are aesthetic and minimalist design, and recognition rather than recall. For the aesthetic and minimalist design, my project does not prioritize the most important information to be shown. I have the text and button displayed all at once, and it can be overwhelming for the user to understand what they have to do first, or where they need to start with the design. Relevant information is not given at the right moment, unless the user commits an error. So, my design is focused on fixing the errors, rather than preventing them. This matters because the design currently overwhelms the user and makes the user not want to engage with the terms and conditions. As for recognition rather than recall, my design kinda violates the heuristic of recognition rather than recall, because of how dense the text is. I could have implemented a stage where it is summarized for the user. Also, the text lacks structure, making it a bit confusing for the reader to follow. This matters because the user can’t quickly understand the terms and conditions, and the point of the project is that they understand it and accept it.

Sketchbook Interface Audit

My sketchbook clearly violates recognition rather than recall. I say this because the sketchbook doesn’t directly tell the user what to do or why they are even on that page. The sketchbook design also confuses the user (even me) on where to click. It feels more like a guessing game when clicking on the links rather than making the user feel confident. This is due to having all the sketch, project, and write-up links so close together, with the same color and font. In conclusion, the sketchbook lacks clear communication and is not easy for the user to understand.

The Hardest Heuristic

Out of the 10 heuristics, I think flexibility and efficiency of use might be the hardest. I think because if the user is a beginner, I am considering students not part of our class, they may not understand the sketchbook or how to interact with the designs that, as a class, we have been able to see the process of development. For beginners, it would mean that I would have to add more instructions or steps to make it easier for beginners to understand, but I can see how it will become very clutterly which can ruin the simplicity of the design. Also, experts such as us students or you, the professor, may expect something more upscale with shortcuts or interesting design choices that may confuse the beginners. In designing, I have to take into consideration both types of people.
