Interface Prototype - Week 1

Prototype - Landing Page

Navigation Model

When it came to designing the mobile and computer interface, I wanted there to be a clear navigation system. This is why, for both the mobile and computer versions, I included a “How to Explore” section so that the user has a better idea of what to expect from the interactive sketchbook.

I made navigation easy by using words and buttons as a form of navigation. When the user clicks on a word, they are moved to the next page. For example, if the user wants to go back to the home page, they can simply click on “HOME.” If the user wants to switch between pictures of Blueberry, they can use the back and forward arrows.

For “Sketch 9: Photos of Blueberry,” I also added white and grey dots so that the user knows which photo in the sequence they are currently viewing.

Hierarchy Logic

When it came to hierarchy, I used four levels for the mobile interface and three for the laptop. For the mobile version, there is the title, headers, body text, and indicator text (forward and back). For the laptop, there is the title, headers, and body text.I also used uppercase and lowercase text so that the user can easily understand the hierarchy, especially for subheaders such as “HOME” and “Sketch 9.”

Mobile-First Reasoning

To be honest, I am glad we switched to designing the mobile prototype first, as I found it to be more complex due to the number of screens required. Since I use my phone more often than my laptop, I was able to rely on my own experience when designing the mobile interface. After figuring out how the screens connected, I could easily translate the design into a laptop interface.

Norman

In reference to Norman, my design does a good job of addressing discoverability and signifiers. Norman emphasized that users should immediately understand what actions are possible without needing instructions. In my design, I added clickable elements such as the triangle button, accept button, and navigation tabs. These are differentiated through grayscale color contrast and placement, such as at the top of the tab section or at the bottom of the screen. There are also dots that indicate which photo in the sequence the user is currently viewing.

Nielsen

In reference to Nielsen, my design aligns with several of Nielsen’s 10 usability heuristics, especially visibility of system status, consistency and standards, and recognition rather than recall. In my design, I included progress dots and confirmation messages (such as the acceptance of the terms and conditions) so that the user always knows what is happening. I also used standard user interface patterns such as icons (a triangle for drop-down menus and arrows for back and forward navigation), so users do not have to relearn interactions. Additionally, my design reduces the user’s need to memorize information because it is easy to navigate, with actions clearly visible at all times.

Shneiderman

In reference to Shneiderman, my design reflects his principles through consistency, informative feedback, and shortcuts. In my design, I use consistent colors—even in grayscale—to distinguish headers and tabs and help users identify what is clickable. There is also consistent typography using uppercase and lowercase text across both mobile and laptop interfaces. My design clearly responds to user actions. On the laptop version, I also included shortcuts to help users navigate more efficiently by using the tab section at the top.
